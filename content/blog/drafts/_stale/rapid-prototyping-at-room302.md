## Rapid Prototyping as Multiplayer Game

_How we turned development into performance art, where creation becomes entertainment and 4 people can outpace entire enterprise teams._

## The Live Coding Session Ritual

Picture this: It's 2pm on a Tuesday. Four of us jump on Discord with a loose idea and whatever energy we've got. Someone's already fired up a shared coding environment. "Let's just see what happens if we..." and suddenly we're all digging into data, chopping CSV files, writing functions in real-time while narrating our thought process.

The energy is infectious. When someone gets stuck, three other brains immediately jump in with solutions. When someone has a breakthrough, everyone can see it happen live. We deploy to R2 or Netlify within the first hour—not because it's done, but because seeing it live adds rocket fuel to the creative process.

Then comes the sketching phase. Still on the call, we're all drawing wireframes, sharing screens, iterating in real-time. "What if we moved this here?" "Oh, that color is perfect." "Wait, I just had an idea..."

It's like playing a cooperative video game where the objective is to build something beautiful.

## Why It Doesn't Feel Like Work

The difference between this and traditional work is night and day. When creation is playful and collaborative, your energy actually INCREASES as you go. Instead of that 3pm crash, you get more excited. Instead of watching the clock, you lose track of time completely.

There's something magical about the real-time dopamine hits: seeing your code work immediately, getting instant feedback from teammates, watching the thing come alive in front of everyone. Traditional development cycles—write code, submit PR, wait for review, iterate—kill this momentum completely.

## The Sherlock Holmes Method

There's something beautifully Victorian about our approach. Most of the time, we're just hanging out—practicing our crafts, experimenting with new tools, sharing interesting links. Like Holmes with his pipe and violin, we're cultivating our minds and staying sharp.

Then someone shows up with a problem. Maybe a client needs data visualization for a complex dataset. Maybe there's a weird API that needs to be tamed. Maybe someone has an idea that just needs to be proven out.

Suddenly, the game is afoot. Four minds that have been casually marinating in curiosity now focus like a laser on the problem. "What if we approached it like this?" "I've seen something similar..." "Oh, I know exactly the tool for this."

We just start throwing ideas around, building on what each other says. Sometimes it works, sometimes it doesn't. But there's no ego about it—if someone's idea doesn't pan out, we just try the next thing.

## Why Small Teams Move Differently

Look, we're not claiming to be revolutionary or anything. It's just that when you've got a few people who trust each other, you can skip a lot of the coordination overhead that bogs down bigger teams.

No one needs to schedule a meeting to discuss whether we should try something. No lengthy docs explaining why an idea might work. We just... try it. If it breaks, we fix it. If it sucks, we try something else.

Maybe it helps that we're all a bit ADHD and get bored easily. The traditional cycle of "plan extensively, then execute" just doesn't match how our brains work. We'd rather build a rough version and see what happens.
